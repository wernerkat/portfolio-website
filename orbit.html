Case Study: Orbit Mobile App

The given challenge was to work with a partner to design a mobile tool to help communities host book-sharing events safely, in a pandemic. No matter how large or small the event, our tool must introduce or increase access to new or used books to school-age children.

Problem

Day 1: After their initial presentation, we got right to work with just 8 days before our prototype was due to be submitted.

Scholastic is the world's largest publisher and distributor of children's books and print and digital educational materials for pre-K to grade 12. A main method they supply books to children is through working directly with schools to host in-person book fairs. When schools closed because of the pandemic, Scholastic Books could not host fairs, and many children lost the opportunity to get new books.

Some of our notes from the brainstorm session

Competitor Analysis

Day 2: Our kickoff meeting took us into the brainstorming phase right away.

There were plenty of products on the market that provide books to children digitally, but they all require that the child has access to a tablet. We wanted our app to have a reach to more children, particularly ones living in book deserts. Furthermore, many required monthly subscriptions which are also not inclusive, since many families won't budget for this.

Our refined problem became "How might we get physical books into the hands of children at a very low (or zero) cost to their families?"

A deep brainstorm session resulted in both my partner and agreeing that the concept of free-little-libraries might offer us a starting point to build our app from. Free-little-libraries are handmade structures that people create to share personal books. They are installed along sidewalks in many communities. With this as our largest inspiration, we started to think about our user.

User Personas

The results of our initial meeting were fruitful. We were able to nail down user personas, user stories and agree upon a hypothesis. Here's how it went:

We knew from Scholastic Book's insight that children who are able to select their own books are more likely to read them, however we also knew that parents were the most important influence in a child's life when it comes to providing access to books.

We decided to target parents with our app, but through gamification and playful design, children will be a secondary target.

User Stories

Being crystal clear about the target user was a major emphasis of the judges when they presented the challenge.

  • As a parent, I need a way to upload books my kids don’t want, so that I can get rid of them.
  • As a parent with a child, I need a place they can pick their own books, so they are more likely to read them.
  • As a parent, I need a way to exchange books, so that I don’t have to spend money.
  • As a parent, I need a way to donate bulk books at no charge, so that I can share them easily.

Hypothesis

By developing a book trading application with functionalities including book search, book upload and, book donate, parents will be able to locate free books for their children easily and pass on books they no longer need in an impactful way.

We will know this is true when parents are successfully trading and donating books.

User Flows

Day 4: At this meeting, we had each prepared flows and low fidelity sketches of two features. Seeing the vision come alive in this stage is one of my favorite parts of the process!

The corresponding features we decided to build based from the user stories were:

  • The process of listing a book available to exchange on the library
  • Going through an exchange request and proposing a trade (including a chat feature)
  • Requesting a form for a mail-in bulk book donation
  • A dashboard including a searchable map to find community hubs where businesses can collect and house books available for free

Day 5

Mid Fidelity Wireframes

Building our mid-fidelity wireframes in the collaborative space of a shared Adobe XD file was an efficient way to collaborate as a team

Through the wireframing process, both flows I was responsible for gained improvements.

  • The ISBN code scanner seemed like the easiest way to take some mental load off of the user when listing a book for trade.
  • The ability to mail in books with a pre-paid label was important to encourage donations. The "book-type" screen was our solution to create a label with the proper weight on it for the post.

Logo

Day 6: While my partner worked through refining our mid-fidelity prototype, I created the logo that would tell the identity of our brand.

Why Orbit? Books could be thought about as "orbiting" around communities as they are traded or stashed at community hubs.

From this logo, blossomed our entire space theme. The excitement of outer space resonates with all ages.

Branding & Gamification

After working through options via moodboards, it was clear we should go in a bold and exciting route for Orbit's look.

Our space theme opened us up to a vibrant color palette. To make Orbit even more inticing for kids, we decided to create a few game-like elements. One of those elements was a customizable avatar feature. When children get a request for a book, and don't see one they want to swap in return, they can earn coins instead.

Final Prototype & Results

Our app was presented to judges at Scholastic Book Fairs and Adobe. It and was awarded first place out of 200 teams!

Conclusion

Orbit solves the problem of providing school-age children better access to books by leveraging the books already collecting dust on shelves right in their neighborhoods. Through free book swapping, community book hub hosting, and bulk book donations, Orbit ensures parents have a way to find free books for their children while building a community around reading.

Learnings:
  • Because we were only given two weeks from start to finish for this project, time management was critical. We had to eliminate certain stages from our process entirely such as user interviews. Instead, we relied on articles and other online sources for supporting data on user needs.
  • When submitting our initial project to the competition, it could not be accompanied by any explanatory dialogue. The app had to be intuitive enough that users could figure it out without us to guide them. This was a key benchmark that we will continue to use to judge effectiveness of our solutions as we design products in the future

Future Ideas:

1. Get the app tested by actual users! (This ideally would have happened sooner, but we unfortunately didn't have the time or means to reach parents and children in our condensed timeline)
2. Dive deeper into accessibility since we didn't have tons of time to explore this with an 8 day timeline
3. Integrate with Scholastic Book Fairs, or another bookseller to purchase books through app
